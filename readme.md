This project analyzes the evolution of global launch cadence from the 1950s to 2025. It explores how the industry transitioned from a government-funded rivalry between two superpowers into a modern, high-frequency "New Space Race" driven by commercial disruption and the rise of China.


The Original Space Race (1950s–1990s)
The initial era of space exploration was defined by intense global activity fueled by the Cold War. Launch volumes surged in the 1950s as the United States and the Soviet Union competed for geopolitical dominance, creating a landscape almost exclusively funded by governments. This rivalry focused heavily on military research, the deployment of early GPS satellites, and foundational scientific exploration. However, following the end of the Cold War, the industry saw a steady decline in global launch volume as the primary political drivers of the 20th-century rivalry faded. While total launch numbers from this period provide a view of overall industry activity, they often obscure the specific entities that were driving that capacity.

The Transfer of Dominance (USA, USSR, & China)
Analyzing the major players reveals a significant shift from a bipolar Cold War struggle to a new multipolar competition. The Soviet Union numerically dominated launch capacity during the 1960s and 1970s, while the United States maintained a steady presence without necessarily dominating the numerical count during the Cold War peak. Following the collapse of the USSR, Russian launch activity entered a period of significant decline. Conversely, China’s growth was initially gradual and consistent but began to accelerate sharply in the 2010s to become a major global player. A massive surge in U.S. activity after 2018 represents a vital transition in the American model, where growth is no longer primarily driven by government activity but by massive commercial expansion.

The Modern Era: Commercial Dominance
The landscape after 2010 represents a total disruption of traditional space models, characterized by government agencies moving toward a "customer model." Following the retirement of the Space Shuttle in 2011, NASA's direct launch activity declined as the agency shifted toward contracting private companies for launch services. While United Launch Alliance (ULA) initially filled this gap, it has seen a slow decline as legacy Atlas rockets retire and the new Vulcan rocket comes online. The true disruptor of this era has been SpaceX, which moved from nearly zero launches in 2010 to over 160 by 2025. Approximately 66% of these missions support the Starlink constellation, with the remainder consisting of government missions and "rideshare" projects for universities and private firms.
At the same time, China has executed a methodical and massive strategic climb. Starting around 2018, China ramped up its operations to reach 86 launches by 2025, establishing itself as the only national entity capable of challenging the sheer volume of the American commercial sector. In contrast, Russia—once the dominant force of the 20th century—has shown a steady and dramatic fade in launch frequency during the 2020s, falling far behind the rapid pace set by the modern leaders in the U.S. and China.

Conclusion: A New Paradigm
The "First Space Race" was defined by pure competitiveness and the singular, symbolic goal of reaching the Moon first. In contrast, the "New Space Race" has shifted the focus toward high-frequency access to orbit, extreme cost efficiency, and the perfection of reusability. This evolution has transformed rockets from expendable government hardware into reusable commercial assets, fundamentally changing how humanity accesses space.
